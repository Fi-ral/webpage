Planet Classifications

As there is no real good system for planet classification in the real world, mostly due to a lack of need for one due to strongly biased data we do have and the lack of direct imaging, this article is my attempt to create one for personal use. While it tries to use the limited data we do have to create a solid basis of categorization, it is by no means comprehensive itself and will certainly have its own issues, but it will suffice in most world-building projects. You are free to use it in any capacity in you wish to use it, with or without modification, and with or without credit.

Basic Categorization

Each planet class consists of at least two components: the planetary mass, indicated by a numeral, and general (surface) features, such as temperature, composition, and atmospheric conditions, indicated by a single uppercase letter. The masses are sorted in ascending order, with larger numbers referring to more massive bodies and lighter bodies corresponding to lower numerals; the general descriptors are, meanwhile, grouped together by shared characteristics into two distinct groups. There may also be one or more optional modifiers for further clarifications in the form of one or more lowercase letters. These are appended to the main category in alphabetical order for clarity and may be separated by a dash should an ambiguous collision occur.

Masses

Type-0

Mass Range: m < 1 * 10^18 kg Type-0 planets are not massive enough to pull themselves into a spherical shape under their own gravity, and the category is excluded solely for the classification of lighter orbital moons without the need for a separate system for these bodies. Its use as a classifier for asteroids and similar objects is, however, not advised.

Type-1

Mass Range: 1 * 10^18 kg < m < 1 * 10^20 kg Type-1 planets are in the transitional space where the body is massive enough to begin to pull itself into a spherical or near-spherical shape, depending on the composition of its core and surface, with bodies made out of ice and other volatiles being more spherical than bodies composed primarily of metals and silicates of the same mass.

Type-2

Mass Range: 1 * 10^20 kg < m < 1 * 10^22 kg Type-2 planets are large enough where, nearly regardless of composition, they will have been able to create a spherical shape but remain substantially lighter than Mercury within the solar system.

Type-3

Mass Range: 1 * 10^22 kg < m < 3 * 10^24 kg Type-3 planets are medium to small worlds, typically consisting of silicates, metals or ices. As they are relatively light, they tend to loose their atmospheres to solar radiation over time and, unless they have tidal interactions with other bodies, do not posses an active core for more than several hundred million years after formation.

Type-4

Mass Range: 3 * 10^24 kg < m < 1 * 10^25 kg Type-4 planets are medium-sized worlds that are able to sustain active cores and atmospheres on long timescales. However, they are usually not massive enough to accrete enough light elements to become either Jovian or Neptunian during their formation, though exceptions are known to exist in the form of chthonian planets.

Type-5

Mass Range: 1 * 10^25 kg < m < 3 * 10^25 kg Type-5 planets are medium to large worlds which are in the transitional phase between having a solid surface and being dominated a dense atmosphere.

Type-6

Mass Range: 3 * 10^25 kg < m < 3 * 10^26 kg Type-6 planets are large worlds, unofficalled called "Subgiants".

Type-7

Mass Range: 3 * 10^26 kg < m < 1 * 10^27 kg Type-7 planets are large worlds, unofficalled called "Giants".

Type-8

Mass Range: 1 * 10^27 kg < m < 1 * 10^28 kg Type-8 planets are large worlds, unofficalled called "Major Giants".

Type-9

Mass Range: > 1 * 10^28 kg Type-9 planets are very large worlds, unoffically called "Supergiants". The higher end of the scale overlaps with brown dwarfs and its usage for objects with internal fusion process is not advised.

General Features

Terrestrial Planets

Terrestrial planets are planets of which their total mass is less than 0.1% hydrogen and helium by content. Atmospheric composition should, if required, use one of the optional atmospheric modifiers.

Type-A

Worlds with a solid crust composed primarily of metals (c > 0.8).

Type-B

Worlds with a solid crust composed primarily of silicates (c > 0.8).

Type-C

Worlds with a solid crust composed primarily of carbides (c > 0.8).

Type-D

Worlds with a solid crust composed primarily of ices (c > 0.8).

Type-E

Worlds with a solid crust composed primarily of organic compounds (c > 0.8).

Type-F

Worlds with a solid crust composed of a mixed composition.

Type-G

Worlds with a liquid crust covering the majority of its surface composed of non-ices.

Type-H

Worlds with a liquid crust covering the majority of its surface composed of ices.

Type-I

Worlds without an identifiable surface from orbit due to permanent or semi-permanent cloud cover.

Gaseous Planets

Gaseous planets are planets of which their total mass is more than 0.1% hydrogen and helium by content. They may be subdivided into Neptunian (0.001 < c < 0.33) and Jovian (c > 0.33), but as their atmospheres consist primarily of lighter elements with trace elements and temperature determining their exact composition, the composition of their mantle and core is not of relevance to this system.

Type-M

Very cold (T < 20K) worlds with a helium atmospheres and hydrogen cloud and ocean system. They are largely featureless dark blue with potential light blue cloud cover around the equator. Due to the extremely low temperatures, they are only found as rouge planets.

Type-N

Very cold (20K < T < 50K) worlds that do not possess any major cloud systems due to a lack of chemicals to condense at these temperatures. They appear as deep blue without notable features outside of storms.

Type-O

Cold (50K < T < 190K) worlds without any major cloud systems. As they lack volatiles, they are a deep blue color.

Type-P

Cold (60K < T < 190K) worlds with major cloud systems of ammonia. Depending on exact composition and temperature, they range from yellow to orange in color.

Type-Q

Cold (50K < T < 175K) worlds with major cloud systems of methane. Depending on exact composition and temperature, they range from light to deep blue in color.

Type-R

Warm (190K < T < 350K) worlds without any major cloud systems. As they lack volatiles, they are a deep blue color.

Type-S

Warm (200K < T < 340K) worlds with major cloud systems of water. Depending on exact composition and temperature, they range fromwhite to very light blue tint.

Type-T

Hot (350K < T < 1200K) worlds without any major cloud systems. As they lack volatiles, they are a deep blue color.

Type-U

Hot (700K < T < 800K) worlds with major cloud systems of alkali metals. Depending on exact composition and temperature, they range from brown to gray in color.

Type-V

Hot (600K < T < 1000K) worlds with major cloud systems of hydrocarbons. Depending on exact composition and temperature, they range from bronze to brown in color.

Type-W

Hot (400K < T < 1000K) worlds with major cloud systems of sulfolian. Depending on exact composition and temperature, they range from orange, over yellow to green in color.

Type-X

Very hot (T > 1200K) worlds made up primarily of hydrogen and helium. They may either possess cloud systems of silicates and other metals or possess none at all.
